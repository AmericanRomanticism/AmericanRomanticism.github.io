"Round our house the grass was green,"

This line is transcendental because it mentions a bit about nature, saying that the grass was green around their house. In context of the song, it means that it was a calming and happy time, much like how Henry David Thoreau was in the Brute Neighbors section of Walden, because usually if grass is green, it's a sign of happiness.

"Choppin' cotton and pusing plows,"

This line is transcendental because it describes a life on the farm in the South. Much like pieces in American Romanticism, it describes and emphasizes a simple American life on the farm, much like the essays written by Ralph Waldo Emerson, especially Self-Reliance.

"And learnin' how to give it all we had."

This line is transcendental because it emphasizes and describes a simple American life. It is very similar to the essay Self-Reliance by Ralph Waldo Emerson, as it emphasizes self-reliance if they learned how to give it all they had.
